01 · Introduction

Hypertension Without Shelter

Hypertension is largely preventable and treatable with inexpensive screening and medications, yet nearly half of United States adults have it and fewer than half achieve control (43.7% in 2017–2018)1–3, at an annual attributable cost of roughly $131 billion.4 That gap prompted the Surgeon General's 2020 Call to Action.5

People experiencing unsheltered homelessness (living in places not intended for human habitation) face a hypertension burden two to four times higher than housed populations.6–10 Cardiovascular disease is the leading cause of death among older homeless men and a top-three cause overall11,12, and all-cause mortality among unsheltered adults approaches ten times that of the general population.13–15

Structural and social factors perpetuate this disparity. Patients must prioritize immediate survival, lack transportation and medication storage, and navigate fragmented, inflexible health systems, all of which delay diagnosis, impede adherence, and prevent follow-up.16–20 Street medicine, delivering care on foot directly to patients in encampments and transitional spaces, is designed to overcome these barriers.28–31 But high-quality data on chronic-disease outcomes, and on the attrition that constrains such programs, remain sparse.

02 · Methods

Cohort & Retention

A retrospective cohort study of encounters documented by Miami Street Medicine from February 2021 through January 2025. Volunteer physicians, nurses, medical students, and patient care coordinators deliver free primary and urgent care in the field and coordinate referrals to primary care, housing, and supportive services, following the Street Medicine Institute's consensus hypertension guidance.36 The University of Miami IRB approved the protocol; reporting follows STROBE.

Eligible participants were adults (≥18 y) experiencing unsheltered homelessness with BP recorded in the EMR. The longitudinal analytic cohort comprised patients with hypertension (documented diagnosis or repeated readings ≥140/90 mmHg) and at least three BP assessments. Multiple same-day readings were averaged into a daily mean.

First-to-final changes were tested with paired t-tests (Wilcoxon confirming diastolic). To model trajectories across all 345 visits while accounting for repeated measures, we fit linear mixed-effects models with time as a fixed effect and patient-level random intercepts. A retention funnel quantified attrition among all screened patients.

04 · Discussion & Conclusions

A Reliable Bridge

Repeated street-based engagement with BP monitoring, medication management, and care coordination was associated with meaningful BP reductions among unsheltered adults. The most robust signal was the modeled diastolic trajectory, which declined consistently across all four mixed-model specifications (P = 0.003–0.009); derived from within-patient longitudinal modeling rather than a first-to-final change from an elevated baseline, it is the finding least vulnerable to regression to the mean.

The whole-cohort systolic reduction of 9.2 mmHg is the primary systolic result, but its meaning is clearest in its distribution: the effect concentrated among the highest-acuity patients rather than spreading uniformly. The pattern indicates a program that manages chronic disease for the patients it retains and concentrates its largest gains on those at highest cardiovascular risk.

Two-thirds never seen again

Two-thirds (65.7%) of patients with stage-2 readings were never re-examined, and only 18.7% reached three visits. Critically, this attrition is not unique to street medicine: in the only randomized comparison in a comparable population, a fixed clinic engaged just 37% of homeless patients over six months, nearly the mirror image of our own return rate. Loss to follow-up here is a system-level barrier, not a defect of the model. For the patients it retains, street medicine delivers chronic disease management that meets conventional benchmarks, so it works best as a reliable bridge to longitudinal care, housing, and social supportive services, not a substitute for them.

A transferable active ingredient

Chart review documented medication access (being unable to fill, store, or afford a prescription) as a barrier in at least a third of encounters, so clinicians often supplied short “bridge” courses on the spot. Point-of-care dispensing paired with social navigation may be a transferable ingredient that does not require the full street medicine apparatus.

In Context

The fair comparison is to other engaged, treated cohorts rather than to population estimates that include the untreated and undiagnosed.

  • 46.2%Our control rate, near the 45.3% reported in a larger LA field-based homeless cohort (n = 887).32
  • −9.2 mmHgWhole-cohort systolic change (−6.8 once RTM-attenuated) sits within the 6–8 mmHg range of pharmacist-led and telemonitoring interventions.40,41
  • −7.1 mmHgTeam-based care with nonphysician medication titration, a comparable benchmark.42
  • ~10%Lower risk of major CV events per 5-mmHg systolic reduction; ~13% each for stroke and heart failure.38

These data include no cost analysis, but directly delivered care has elsewhere been tied to less emergency reliance: a Boston mobile clinic returned an estimated $1.30 per dollar invested46, and a housing and case management trial cut ED visits 24% and hospitalizations 29%.48 The financing levers are arriving too: Medicaid street medicine billing pathways, a dedicated place-of-service code, value-based and braided funding, and municipal contracts.

Limitations

No control group. A retrospective evaluation without a contemporaneous comparison; secular trends cannot be excluded.

Selection from attrition. 75.5% of screened patients had a single visit; cohort outcomes reflect change among the more engaged.

Regression to the mean. Baseline systolic was inversely correlated with change (r = −0.61); the modeled within-patient trajectory (~−3 mmHg) was far smaller than the −20.7 mmHg endpoint change in the same high-risk patients.

Field measurement & missingness. Street BP is more variable than office readings; mixed models assume data missing at random, uncertain in a transient population where dropout may be informative.

“

Street medicine is not a population health strategy; it reliably manages chronic disease for the patients it retains and specializes in reaching the sickest, serving as a high-intensity bridge to longitudinal care, housing, and social supportive services.
